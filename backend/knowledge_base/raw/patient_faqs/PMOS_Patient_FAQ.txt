PMOS (formerly PCOS) - Patient Information and Frequently Asked Questions

SOURCE NOTE: This plain-language summary is compiled from patient-education material consistent with ACOG, Mayo Clinic, and NIH guidance. It is for education only and is not medical advice. For official patient handouts, see acog.org, mayoclinic.org, and nichd.nih.gov.

WHAT IS PMOS/PCOS?

PMOS (Polyendocrine Metabolic Ovarian Syndrome), until recently called PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome), is one of the most common hormone conditions affecting people with ovaries during their reproductive years. It involves the hormones and the metabolism, not only the ovaries. The name was updated in May 2026 to better reflect that it affects the whole body. If you were diagnosed with PCOS, that diagnosis is still valid - it is the same condition with a new name.

WHAT ARE THE COMMON SIGNS?

Common signs include irregular periods (periods that come infrequently, unpredictably, or not at all), signs of higher androgen levels such as extra hair growth on the face or body, acne, or scalp hair thinning, and difficulty getting pregnant. Some people also notice weight changes or find weight hard to manage. Not everyone has every sign, and you do not need to have ovarian cysts to have the condition.

HOW IS IT DIAGNOSED?

Doctors commonly use a set of guidelines called the Rotterdam criteria. A diagnosis is generally considered when at least two of these three things are present: irregular or absent ovulation (often seen as irregular periods), signs of higher androgen levels (either physical signs or a blood test), and a particular appearance of the ovaries on ultrasound. Your doctor will also rule out other conditions that can look similar, such as thyroid problems, before confirming the diagnosis.

WHAT LIFESTYLE CHANGES HELP?

Lifestyle steps are usually the first thing recommended. A balanced eating pattern and regular physical activity can help with symptoms, especially those linked to insulin and metabolism. For people carrying extra weight, even a modest loss of around 5 to 10 percent of body weight can improve periods, symptoms, and fertility. There is no single "PMOS diet" that works best for everyone - the most helpful plan is one you can keep up with over time. Tracking your cycle in an app can be a useful way to show patterns to your doctor.

CAN I STILL GET PREGNANT?

Many people with PMOS/PCOS do conceive, sometimes naturally and sometimes with help. If irregular ovulation is making it harder, doctors can offer treatments that encourage ovulation. If pregnancy is a goal, it is worth discussing options early with a healthcare provider.

IS IT SERIOUS LONG-TERM?

PMOS/PCOS is a lifelong condition and is linked with higher chances of things like insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, and heart-related risks over time. The encouraging part is that regular check-ins, healthy habits, and appropriate treatment can lower these risks and help you feel better day to day.

WHAT ABOUT EMOTIONAL WELLBEING?

Living with PMOS/PCOS can affect mood and quality of life, and feelings of anxiety or low mood are common. This is a recognised part of the condition, not a personal failing. If you are struggling, it is worth raising with your doctor, who can screen for this and point you toward support.

WHEN SHOULD I SEE A DOCTOR PROMPTLY?

Reach out to a healthcare provider if your periods are very irregular or absent, if you have troubling symptoms like unwanted hair growth or persistent acne, or if you are having trouble getting pregnant. Seek urgent care for severe pelvic pain or unusually heavy bleeding.
